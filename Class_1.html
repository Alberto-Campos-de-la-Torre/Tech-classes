Topic Development: Key Concepts

What is a Digital Citizen?

Being a digital citizen means more than just knowing how to use technology. It's about participating in the online world thoughtfully, safely, and ethically. It's about understanding that our online actions have real-world consequences for ourselves and others. According to the ISTE standards, a good digital citizen focuses on several key areas:

  • Digital Identity and Reputation: This is the persona you create online. A responsible digital citizen is aware that their posts, comments, and interactions are often permanent and contribute to how others see them. They actively work to build a positive and authentic online presence.
  • Positive and Ethical Behavior: This involves treating others with respect and kindness online, just as you would in person. It means avoiding cyberbullying, hate speech, and spreading negativity. It also means acting legally and respecting online rules and laws.
  • Rights and Intellectual Property: This is about understanding that content online (music, images, articles, videos) is owned by someone. A good digital citizen gives credit to creators, asks for permission before using content, and understands concepts like copyright and fair use.
  • Privacy and Data Management: In the digital age, our personal information is valuable. A responsible digital citizen knows how to protect their private data (passwords, addresses, phone numbers) and is aware of how websites and apps collect and use their information.

Your Digital Footprint in Detail

Your digital footprint is the complete set of data you leave behind online. It's crucial to understand its two main types:

  • Active Footprint: This is the data you share intentionally. Examples include sending an email, posting on social media, filling out an online form, or leaving a comment on a blog. You have direct control over this part of your footprint.
  • Passive Footprint: This is the data collected without your direct action or awareness. Examples include websites tracking your IP address to know your location, apps collecting data on your usage habits, or cookies that track your browsing activity across different sites. While less visible, this data creates a detailed profile of your online behavior.

Both types of footprints combine to create your overall digital identity, which can be seen by friends, family, teachers, and even future employers or universities.

Main Activity: Digital Footprint Analysis of a Public Figure

Time to investigate like a digital detective! You will analyze the digital footprint of a person of public interest.

  1. Choose a public figure you are interested in (a scientist, artist, athlete, activist, etc.).
  2. Research their digital footprint: Use search engines like Google to find information. Look at news sites, online encyclopedias (like Wikipedia), official websites, published interviews, etc.
  3. Analyze your findings: What kind of information do you find? What image does this person project online? Is their digital footprint mostly positive, negative, or neutral? What strategy do they seem to follow to build their public image?
  4. Create an analysis document: Prepare a document (in Google Docs or Word) where you answer the questions above. Include links to your sources as evidence.
  5. Write a conclusion: At the end of your document, add a paragraph with your personal conclusion. What do you think of this public figure's digital footprint? Do you think they manage it well? What did you learn from this analysis?
  6. Submission: Upload your final document to the corresponding assignment in Google Classroom.
